10 Frequently Asked National Parks Questions

Is it okay to feed the animals?
No, wild animals are very dangerous. You should keep your distance.
What if they look friendly?
They are not. Even the cute ones
Why would God make bears look so huggable if I weren't supposed to hug one?
You'll have to ask God that one. We are straying off topic. This is an FAQ about the whole park, not just the wildlife.
Can I start a fire?
You can start a fire, as long as you put it out. That is a perfectly acceptable outdoor activity. Unless we are under a fire watch, which happens more and more because we're rolling back regulations on industry... ah, this isn't the place. Next question.
Who owns the National Forest?
Technically, if you pay taxes... You do!
So all of the huggable bears and puppy wolves are mine?
No...
But you said I own them.
Not a question, but I'll answer anyway. We share the land. The animals, however, are wild and can be governed by no man.
Who is going to stop me if I domesticate, say, a bald eagle?
The eagle may try and stop you. With it's very sharp talons.
Why are there so many rules around what we can and can't do with the wildlife.
Every rule in the National Forest is written in blood. The park rangers will not always be there to enforce said rules. Do with that info what you must.
So I CAN feed the animals?
Do what you must, but don't say I didn't warn you.

Lyndon B. Johnson National Historical Park

from parktrust.org

  • Lyndon B. Johnson National Historical Park preserves the story of the life of the 36th U.S. president
  • It includes his reconstructed birthplace, boyhood home, grandfather's house, grade school, and the ranch and house in Blanco, TX, often called the “Texas White House.”
  • In 1972, the Johnsons donated the Blanco ranch to NPS and the American people
  • After the president died in 1973, his wife, Lady Bird Johnson, continued to live at the ranch part-time until her death in 2007

Lake Meredith National Recreation Area

from parktrust.org

  • National Park Trust worked with the National Park Service Regional Office in Denver to acquire the last two private properties inside the official boundary of Lake Meredith National Recreation Area
  • The National Park Service uses funds from the Land and Water Conservation Fund (LWCF) to buy land in and adjacent to parks critical to the protection of parks’ natural and cultural resources
  • The land on a mesa overlooking Lake Meredith is part of the story of human habitation there that goes back more than 13,000 years
  • Most notable is evidence of the Antelope Creek people, who occupied the area between AD 1150 and 1450

San Antonio Missions National Historical Park

from parktrust.org

  • San Antonio Missions National Historical Park preserves and protects four of five Spanish Colonial Missions around the city
  • The oldest dates from the early 1700s; the fifth mission, not in the park, is the Alamo
  • The property is part of the area around the missions that was cultivated and fed by the historic canals or acequias
  • The east edge of the property includes a section of the San Juan Acequia
